Stop Using Perplexity for Business Until You Read Their Terms of Service

Perplexity AI's updated terms restrict all non-Enterprise users to personal, non-commercial use — even if you're paying for Pro — and grant the company broad, irrevocable rights over your content.

Quick Answer: Perplexity AI's Terms of Service grant the company a royalty-free, transferable, sublicensable, and irrevocable license to all user-submitted content. Non-Enterprise users are restricted to personal, non-commercial use only — meaning paid Pro subscribers cannot legally use outputs for business purposes. Unlike OpenAI and Anthropic, Perplexity does not assign output ownership to users.

What Does Perplexity's License Actually Say?

If you've been using Perplexity for your business — client research, marketing copy, competitive analysis — you need to read this carefully.

Perplexity's Terms of Service include language that most users never see. When you use their platform, you're granting Perplexity a license to "access, use, host, cache, store, reproduce, transmit, display, publish, distribute, and modify" your content. And those rights are described as "royalty free, transferable, sub-licensable, worldwide and irrevocable."

Let's break that down in plain English. Royalty-free means they don't pay you. Transferable means they can hand those rights to someone else. Sublicensable means they can give those rights to third parties. And irrevocable means you can't take it back — for as long as your content is stored with them.

That's not a summary or interpretation. That's their actual contract language, directly from their published Terms of Service.

For any small business owner or content creator uploading original work — strategy documents, proprietary research, client deliverables — this means Perplexity retains the right to use, modify, or distribute that content without compensating you.

Even if you're thinking "they probably wouldn't enforce that," the legal exposure is real. If a client's proprietary data ends up in Perplexity's training pipeline or gets redistributed to a third-party partner, you have no contractual recourse. You already agreed to let them do it.

Can You Use Perplexity for Business at All?

Here's where it gets worse for the millions of professionals who rely on Perplexity daily: the platform is now explicitly limited to personal, non-commercial use. This applies to every subscription tier except Enterprise — including the paid Pro plan.

That means using Perplexity Pro to draft marketing materials, analyze competitors, generate client-facing content, or do anything work-related technically violates their terms. You're paying for a premium product that you're contractually prohibited from using for the very thing most people buy it for.

Compare that to how other platforms handle this. OpenAI's terms state plainly: "We hereby assign to you all our right, title, and interest, if any, in and to Output." Anthropic's terms say essentially the same. Google Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, and Adobe Firefly all offer IP indemnity on their business tiers.

Perplexity? They state: "The Company does not claim any ownership in Your Content." But they also don't assign you ownership of their outputs. That legal gap is significant — it means the content you generate may not clearly belong to anyone, which creates real risk if you ever need to defend your work commercially.

This is a pattern I see across the AI industry — platforms quietly updating their terms knowing most users will never check. Perplexity isn't doing anything illegal. But they are counting on the fact that their users won't read the fine print. If you're a business owner or content creator, you need to treat every AI platform's Terms of Service like a contract — because that's exactly what it is. And right now, Perplexity's contract is one of the least favorable in the industry for individual users.

The Enterprise tier tells a different story. Under Perplexity's Enterprise Terms, customers retain all ownership rights in both inputs and outputs, and Perplexity explicitly assigns any rights it may obtain back to the customer. But that tier isn't available to individual users, freelancers, or small business owners — the very people most likely to be affected by these restrictions.
